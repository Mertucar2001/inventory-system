# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

A single owner-operator of Mertory, a Berlin-based Anlasser & Lichtmaschinen workshop (starter motors and alternators), runs the whole business through this tool — ordering, stocking, and selling parts, and tracking customers, without other staff or separate logins. They use it from the counter or the shop floor, often mid-task with a customer waiting or a car on the lift, and the screen is viewed under variable, sometimes bright, workshop lighting rather than a calibrated office monitor.

The business sells and repairs remanufactured (not just new) starter motors and alternators, following the auto-parts industry's standard core-exchange model: a customer trades in their old/failed unit for a remanufactured one, and that core is worth a real deposit (typically €75–200) since it goes back to the rebuilder for credit. The tool tracks which sales still have a core outstanding, because that's real money the shop is owed and easy to lose track of without a system. Parts aren't limited to passenger cars either — the shop serves trucks, motorcycles, buses, and agricultural/construction machinery, so every part is categorized by vehicle type alongside its free-text make/model.

## Product Purpose

This tool tracks parts inventory, sales, and customer records for Mertory's starter-motor and alternator business. It exists to replace paper and memory with something that stays accurate under real working conditions. Success means fewer stock and selling errors (never selling a part that isn't there), reliable buy/sell margin tracking for basic bookkeeping, and day-to-day operations — checking stock, logging a sale, finding a customer — that take seconds, not minutes.

The interface is trilingual — Turkish (default, the owner's own language), German, and English — with German held to the same full-coverage bar as Turkish, since most walk-in customers in Berlin speak German rather than Turkish. A language switch lives in the header on every page and in a dedicated Ayarlar (Settings) destination; the choice is remembered per device.

## Positioning

One tool that keeps parts stock, sales, and customer records linked — a sale updates stock and ties to a customer automatically, no manual reconciling — fast enough to use mid-task at the counter, without the overhead of real accounting software.

## Brand Personality

Sturdy & no-fuss. Plain, dependable, nothing decorative standing between the owner and the task. It should feel like a good hand tool — and like Mertory's own oval badge: navy and orange, the kind of mark that's been stamped on starter-motor and alternator parts for decades. Built for the job, not for show.

## Anti-references

Not the generic AI-template look — purple gradients, rounded cards everywhere, stock icon soup. Not the current stock Bootstrap blue/gray default theme it ships with today; it should read as designed, not templated. Not a bloated SaaS dashboard crowded with charts and widgets that don't matter for a daily counter workflow. Not an invented color palette either — navy and orange are Mertory's real brand colors, from its own oval badge; nothing gets substituted or added alongside them.

## Design Principles

Clarity over decoration: every screen answers "how much stock, what did I sell, who's this customer" at a glance, with nothing ornamental competing for attention.

Legible under real conditions: contrast holds up in bright daylight or a dim garage, not just on a calibrated screen — this is the working test, not the WCAG minimum.

Speed for one pair of hands: a single operator can log a sale or check stock in seconds, mid-task, without hunting through menus or confirming unnecessary dialogs.

Linked, not siloed: stock, sales, and customers stay visibly connected on screen so nothing needs manual reconciling between them.

Plain-spoken, not corporate: utilitarian tone, no filler copy, no forced personality in labels or empty states.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No formal WCAG target requested. Prioritize strong, glare-resistant contrast — body text meaningfully above the 4.5:1 floor — since the screen is read under variable and sometimes bright workshop lighting. Favor comfortably sized tap targets and forgiving hit areas for quick, occasionally one-handed use at the counter.
